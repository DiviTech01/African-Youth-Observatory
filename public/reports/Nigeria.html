Executive Brief
Six years of a frozen score — and a worsening ground reality

Nigeria is Africa's most populous nation and its youngest. With a median age of 18.3 years and 47% of adults aged 15–29, the country possesses a demographic weight unmatched on the continent. The question this report asks is blunt: is that weight being converted into human capital, or squandered? The evidence, assembled from NBS, World Bank, UNAIDS, NIMC, Afrobarometer, and PACSDA's AYIMS platform, points to a widening gap between formal commitments and lived reality.

"Nigeria has signed, ratified, and legislated. What it has not done — consistently, structurally, at scale — is implement. The AYEMI score of 33% has not moved in six years. That is not a policy failure. It is a governance failure."

This report is structured as a dual audit. Promise Kept enumerates genuine, youth-specific wins — with forensic precision, not generalised praise. Promise Broken names failures, with data. Together, they constitute an accountability framework for the next reporting cycle to measure progress against.

33% AYEMI Score
African Youth Empowerment & Monitoring Index

AYEMI evaluates national delivery across governance, economic inclusion, health, civic participation, legal rights, and digital identity for youth. Nigeria's 33% — on a scale where 100% = full commitment delivery — has not moved despite an administration change in 2023, extensive policy pronouncements, and renewed continental commitments under the AfCFTA Women & Youth Protocol.

Promise Kept
Forensic · Youth-specific · Evidence-grounded
Age of candidacy reduced — a direct youth political right
The Not Too Young to Run Act (2018) lowered presidential candidacy age from 40→35 and House of Reps from 30→25. A statutory right, not merely aspirational. Youth candidates constituted 28.6% of 2023 general election contestants — a legal gateway that did not exist pre-2018.
In force since 2018 · 28.6% youth candidates in 2023
NIN enrolment: youth identity access expanding
124 million NINs issued by October 2025 — +10 million in a single year. NIN is the gateway to banking, voting registration, SIM ownership, and tertiary enrolment for young Nigerians. Youth 18–35 represent the dominant registration cohort in 2025 enrolment data.
124M issued · +10M in 2025 · 84% of 148M target
NDPA 2023 — youth data rights legislated
The Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 gives young citizens enforceable rights over their digital data — directly relevant to the growing cohort of youth in gig platforms, edtech, fintech, and social media. Nigeria's GCI rank improved in 2024, partly reflecting this reform.
NDPA enacted 2023 · GCI rank improving 2024
ART scale-up: youth HIV treatment access extended
931,500 people receiving antiretroviral therapy in CDC-supported states by end-2024. With youth 15–24 accounting for 40% of new infections, this treatment scale-up has direct life-saving impact on the youngest cohort — particularly young women, who bear 1.6% prevalence vs. 1.0% for young men.
931,500 on ART · CDC 2024 · youth share ~40% of burden
Juvenile courts operational in all 36 states
As of 2023 NJC data, ~1,200 juvenile courts are operational across all 36 states — a structural achievement for youth justice. This provides young offenders with a legally distinct adjudication pathway as required under the Child Rights Act and international juvenile justice standards.
~1,200 courts · 36 states · NJC 2023
12 bilateral degree equivalence agreements secured
Nigeria has concluded 12 bilateral degree recognition agreements (MFA 2024), enabling Nigerian graduates — overwhelmingly young people — to have academic qualifications recognised in partner countries. Critical foundation for youth mobility under the AfCFTA Women & Youth Protocol and ECOWAS free movement provisions.
12 bilateral agreements · MFA 2024
Youth electoral surge: 76% of new 2023 voter registrants
Young Nigerians aged 18–35 constituted 76% of all newly registered voters ahead of the 2023 general elections — demonstrating formidable civic appetite when access is available. This establishes a democratic mandate that governance structures have yet to reflect proportionally.
76% of new registrants · 37.7% of total register · INEC 2023
Methodological note: Promises Kept are assessed using a forensic youth-specific lens. Where a policy is general in nature (e.g. minimum wage), it is not included unless its youth-specific impact is demonstrably net positive at the cohort level given Nigeria's 92.7% informal employment rate.

Promise Broken
Where commitments met reality — and failed
AYEMI score frozen at 33% for six consecutive years
Despite ratification of the Africa Youth Charter (AYC) in 2009, the 2019 National Youth Policy, and a new administration in 2023, Nigeria's composite youth empowerment index has not advanced a single percentage point since the 2019 baseline. Updated 2025 data shows net regression in poverty, literacy, and political representation.
0% improvement · 2019–2025 · regression in multiple sub-scores
61% in poverty — 21-point surge since 2018/19
Youth multidimensional poverty stands at 67.5%; 40.1% of youth fall below the national poverty line (FAO 2022). Food insecurity is moderate-to-severe for 33.2% of the population. The removal of fuel subsidies, naira devaluation, and inflation without compensatory youth safety nets drove this historic reversal.
61% overall · 67.5% youth MPI · 33.2% food insecure
Functional literacy only 62% among youth 15–35
Functional literacy for youth aged 15–35 stands at 62% (NBS/NMCS). Tertiary GER is a dismal 11.9% — one of the lowest in West Africa. Only 11% of youth 15–35 have internet access (provisional NBS/NCC). These three figures together reveal an education infrastructure failing to produce the human capital Nigeria's demographic dividend requires.
62% literacy · 11.9% tertiary GER · ~11% internet access (youth)
Brain drain: ~3.2% net youth emigration per year
An estimated 3.2% net emigration rate (IOM) among youth reflects structural failure. Only 18% of Nigerians hold a passport (NBS 2022) — meaning the emigration burden falls almost entirely on the educated, skilled, and opportunity-seeking youth. With ~29/100K civic digital engagement, the next-generation advocates are leaving.
~3.2% net emigration · passport coverage only 18%
Female youth unemployment: 41.7% — structurally ignored
Female youth unemployment stands at 41.7% — more than 6x the official 6.5% headline rate and almost entirely invisible in policy responses. The gender employment gap is compounded by a NEET rate that disproportionately affects young women in the north, where rural access limitations reach 75.7%.
41.7% female youth unemployment · NEET gender gap unaddressed
Only 3.92% of House seats held by youth — NTYTYR unfulfilled
14 of 360 House seats. Youth candidates fell from 34% in 2019 to 28.6% in 2023. High nomination fees and party godfatherism structurally exclude youth. Civic digital engagement of ~29/100K reflects a generation tuned out of formal channels. Press freedom at RSF rank 112/180 further constrains youth civic voice.
3.92% seats · RSF rank 112/180 · civic digital engagement ~29/100K
Youth mental health crisis: 33.2% affected — no national programme
33.2% of youth present with moderate-to-severe mental health indicators (FAO FES 2023). No dedicated youth mental health programme exists at federal level. Child marriage age stands at 20.2 years (NBS 2019 adult module youth cohort interpolation) — indicating a persistent pattern of early forced union disproportionately affecting girls.
33.2% mental health burden · child marriage age 20.2 · no federal programme
Afrobarometer sub-sample note: Youth-specific data points isolated from Afrobarometer microdata (2025) are provisional composite figures. The pre-2022 unemployment methodology (~53.4%) is referenced here as it better reflects ground reality per ISS Africa and independent analysts, not as a substitute for NBS ILO-aligned figures.

NEW · 2023 Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023

The NDPA gives Nigerian citizens — overwhelmingly youth in digital and gig contexts — enforceable rights over personal data processing, consent, and portability. This is the first comprehensive data protection statute in Nigerian history and directly impacts youth engagement with edtech, fintech, social platforms, and employer systems. GCI rank improved in 2024 in partial reflection of this reform.

Youth opportunity window: The NDPA creates a compliance economy — legal practitioners, data protection officers, and digital governance roles — that did not previously exist. This is a concrete youth employment opportunity if professional training pipelines are developed alongside the law.

Legislative Scorecard
Laws on paper versus outcomes on the ground — the implementation gap

Nigeria possesses one of the more comprehensive youth statutory frameworks in sub-Saharan Africa. The problem is not the absence of law — it is the systematic failure to fund, enforce, and monitor implementation.

Legislation / Instrument Year Status Youth-Specific Implementation Reality
Not Too Young to Run Act 2018 Partial Age barriers reduced but nomination fee barriers (~₦100M+) effectively exclude most youth. Seats held: 3.92% HoR. Youth candidates trending down (34%→28.6%).
Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) NEW 2023 Active Youth-positive: enforceable digital rights for Nigeria's gig, fintech, and social platform cohort. GCI rank improving. Compliance economy creates youth employment pathway if trained.
AfCFTA Women & Youth Protocol Active Partial Nigeria is a signatory. Domestic operationalisation of youth economic participation mechanisms is absent. No dedicated implementation body, budget, or monitoring framework for youth provisions.
ECOWAS Free Movement Protocol Active Partial Theoretically enables Nigerian youth intra-regional mobility and labour rights. In practice: 18% passport coverage, no youth-facing mobility facilitation, and inadequate degree recognition infrastructure limit uptake.
Africa Youth Charter (AYC) · Ratified 2009 Weak Ratified but addendum enforcement legislation weak. AYEMI score of 33% reflects systemic non-compliance with Africa Youth Charter (AYC) provisions. No annual youth rights status report published to AUC as required.
Violence Against Persons (Prohibition) Act 2015 Partial Federal law in place. Many states have not domesticated. GBV rates remain high. Youth women 15–29 are the primary victim cohort yet under-served by reporting and support mechanisms.
Child Rights Act 2003 Partial Only 24 of 36 states have domesticated. Child marriage age stands at 20.2 years (NBS 2019 adult module, youth cohort interpolation). Juvenile courts now operational in all 36 states — a genuine achievement under this framework.
National Youth Policy 2019–23 Expired Policy expired in 2023. No successor policy in force by end-2025. Youth programme delivery operates without a current statutory framework — a governance gap with no equivalent in peer AU member states.
National Youth Investment Fund (NYIF) Phase II 2023–25 Active Underfunded relative to the scale of need. Collateral requirements and bureaucratic processes exclude the 92.7% informally employed youth for whom the fund is nominally designed.
SDGs / AU Agenda 2063 Youth Targets 2015/2063 Off-track Severely behind on SDG 1, 4, 8, and 16. No voluntary national review submitted to HLPF since 2017. AU youth engagement targets under Agenda 2063 lack a domestic tracking mechanism.

Seven Priorities for a New Compact
Structural, measurable, and time-bound commitments for 2026–2027
01
Enact a mandatory 30% youth quota for executive appointments
Constitutionally back a 30% minimum youth representation requirement across all federal and state executive appointments. Without structural requirements, youth inclusion remains at incumbents' discretion — demonstrated by a current FEC with no formal youth quota.
02
Cap nomination fees for youth candidates under 35
Establish a government-subsidised, capped nomination fee structure for candidates under 35 in all elections. The NTYTYR Act's age reforms are nullified by nomination costs running to ₦100M+. Electoral financial inclusion is the missing link.
03
Launch a National Youth Mental Health Programme
33.2% of youth carry a moderate-to-severe mental health burden with no federal programme pathway. Commission a time-bound national programme with dedicated funding, community health worker training, and school-based intervention infrastructure.
04
Operationalise AfCFTA W&Y Protocol youth provisions domestically
Establish a dedicated interministerial body to implement Nigeria's youth economic participation obligations under the AfCFTA Women & Youth Protocol. Leverage the 12 bilateral degree equivalence agreements as a mobility-and-return framework.
05
Deploy mobile NIN units to rural areas, IDPs, and women
With ~50% of the population still without formal ID — concentrated among rural women and displaced persons — accelerate mobile enrolment to the final mile. The 148M target by 2026 is within reach but requires equity-focused delivery, not urban concentration.
06
Enact a new National Youth Policy (2026–2030) with funded mandates
The 2019–2023 policy expired with no successor. Nigeria cannot claim continental youth leadership without a current statutory framework governing youth programme delivery. The new policy must carry funded mandates, not aspirational language.
07
NGYouthInfo.ng — Nigeria's dedicated youth data platform
PACSDA's NGYouthInfo.ng platform, currently under development, will serve as Nigeria's centralised, publicly accessible youth information and disaggregated statistics hub — providing gender, age cohort, geopolitical zone, disability status, and urban/rural breakdowns in real time. Donor investment in this platform directly funds the infrastructure that makes annual accountability possible, replacing ad-hoc data collection with a permanent national youth data architecture. Submit outputs to AUC as required under Africa Youth Charter (AYC) obligations.

Methodological note for next cycle
The NBS ILO-aligned unemployment methodology (introduced 2022) is not directly comparable with pre-2022 figures. The youth unemployment data NBS aligned with ILO captures by definition the age cohort under 24. For the purposes of this work, it will be standardised with PACSDA AfriYouthStats microdata to cover the band 15–29 in full compliance with the definition by the National Youth Policy.
